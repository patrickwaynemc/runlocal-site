Why run for local office?

Local offices shape schools, roads, budgets, and public safety — decisions that touch daily life more than almost anything in Washington.

🏫

Real, Local Impact

Change is faster and more visible in your own community than at the federal level.

🌱

A Lower Barrier to Entry

Most local races have small budgets, short ballots, and no career-politician gatekeepers.

🤝

Builds Future Leaders

Local office is where most state and national leaders got their start.

📣

Underrepresented Voices Needed

Many local seats go uncontested each cycle — your candidacy could be the only choice voters get.

Your roadmap to running

Every state's rules differ slightly, but nearly every local candidacy follows this path.

1

Pick your office & check eligibility

Confirm residency, age, and citizenship requirements for the seat with your county/city clerk or Secretary of State site.

2

Research the seat

  • Term length & election calendar
  • Current officeholder & past vote totals
  • Filing deadline & signature requirements
3

File your candidacy

Submit your declaration of candidacy, pay any filing fee or gather the required nominating signatures with your local election office.

4

Build your team & message

Recruit a treasurer and a few volunteers. Write a simple platform: 3 issues you genuinely care about and can act on locally.

5

Set up campaign finance

Open a campaign bank account, register with your local election/finance board, and track every dollar from day one.

6

Get in front of voters

Door-knock, attend community meetings, show up at candidate forums, and use low-cost local social media and yard signs.

7

Get out the vote

In the final weeks, focus entirely on turning out your identified supporters — local races are often decided by a handful of votes.

Which office is right for you?

Local government has more entry points than people realize.

School Board

Curriculum, budgets, and school policy. Often nonpartisan with low signature thresholds.

City / Town Council

Zoning, local ordinances, budgets, and public services for your municipality.

County Commission

Roads, public health, parks, and county-level budgeting decisions.

Mayor

Executive leadership for a city or town — higher visibility, higher stakes.

Library / Park District Board

Smaller, often overlooked boards that are a great first step into public service.

Judge / Clerk / Sheriff

Many states elect these roles locally — check your state's specific requirements.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a political background to run?

No. Most local candidates are teachers, small business owners, parents, and neighbors. Genuine care about your community matters more than experience.

How much does it cost to run?

Many local races can be run on a few hundred to a few thousand dollars — often just filing fees, yard signs, and printing costs.

How do I find my filing deadline?

Check your Secretary of State's elections page or call your county/city clerk's office directly — deadlines vary widely by state and office.

Can I run without a political party?

Yes. Many local offices are officially nonpartisan, and independent candidates can run in most jurisdictions with enough signatures.

What's the single most important first step?

Call your local election/clerk office and ask for the candidate filing packet for your specific office — it answers 90% of logistical questions.
